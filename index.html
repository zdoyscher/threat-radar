IMPACT — WHAT IT MEANS FOR THE MONEY

Fee compression at the root, hitting all three segments at once. The battleground is the mid-market — startups, agencies, and independent operators willing to route work to whatever is cheapest and good enough — and "cheapest" now spans "free on the desktop app" to "already included in the Microsoft 365 licence you're paying for anyway." That's the killer: there is no procurement decision to win, no trial to lose. The buyer-side surveys are unambiguous. The mechanism is documented — AI compressed the junior-analyst labour that justified pyramid pricing, and firms captured the gain as internal margin instead of passing it through, which is exactly why the client eventually asks for it back.

CONTEXT — WHAT "OPEN WEIGHTS" ACTUALLY MEANS FOR A NON-TECHNICAL OWNER

Ignore the parameter counts, and ignore the sold-out headline. Here is the only mechanic that matters. Until now, when your client used a cheap AI tool, that tool was renting its intelligence from OpenAI, Anthropic or Google — companies with shareholders, compute bills, and every incentive to raise prices over time. That was quietly your friend: it put a floor under how cheap the substitute for your work could get. Open weights remove the landlord. When Moonshot publishes K3 on July 27, any tool-maker or hosting company can run a top-four model themselves and charge whatever covers electricity — and the fact that Moonshot's own API sold out is irrelevant, because the point of open weights is that nobody has to use Moonshot's servers. You will not see this as "my client downloaded a 2.8-trillion-parameter model" — nobody's client is doing that. You will see it as the $79/month tool in your category quietly getting much better at the thing you charge $4,000 for, and never getting more expensive. DeepSeek already shows the endpoint: V4-Flash runs at $0.14 per million input tokens and $0.28 out; V4-Pro's standing rate is $0.435/$0.87. For scale, a 5,000-word strategy document is on the order of a few cents of model cost.

IMPACT — WHAT IT MEANS FOR THE MONEY

This threat reaches you indirectly, which is exactly why owners under-rate it — and why for weeks it sat below the flashier stories. Nobody's client is self-hosting a 2.8-trillion-parameter model; the r/LocalLLaMA consensus is that almost no one can run K3 locally, and the GPU crunch that sold out Moonshot's own API underlines how compute-heavy it still is today. The transmission is through the tools. Every content platform, chatbot, research assistant, ad-creative generator and "AI strategist" product in your client's stack buys intelligence wholesale and resells it. When wholesale collapses toward zero and can never rise again, three things follow. First, the cheap tier gets good. The $20–100/mo tools that already replace a content retainer stop being a downgrade and start being a defensible choice. Second, your competitors' costs fall to yours. Any advantage you built on "I have the good model and my competitor doesn't" evaporates — everyone has it. Third, and most uncomfortable: the price of output becomes public knowledge. Your client can look up what a million tokens costs. When the raw material of a deliverable is measurably worth cents, an invoice that prices the deliverable itself becomes indefensible — not because your work is worthless, but because you priced the wrong thing. The firms that survive this will be selling the decision, the accountability, and the relationship. The ones that don't will be selling documents in a market where documents are free.

▶ PLAN OF ACTION
Stop selling the output. Start selling the decision about the output.
  1. Price one deliverable in tokens this week. Take your most-produced artifact, generate it with a cheap model, and work out the raw model cost. Seeing "$0.11" next to a line item you bill at $1,200 is unpleasant and clarifying. That gap is your value — it had better be describable as something other than the document.
  2. Move your own delivery onto the cheap tier deliberately. If a $0.14-per-million model handles first drafts, transcription, summarising and research at acceptable quality, your cost of goods falls while your price holds. Do this before a competitor does it and undercuts you with the savings.
  3. Kill volume-based pricing lines. Anything priced per post, per page, per report, per hour of production is priced against a collapsing input. Convert those to a flat fee tied to an outcome, or to a retainer for judgment and accountability — before renewal season, not after.
  4. Watch July 27, then look at your category's tools 60 days later. Don't panic on release day; nothing changes that morning, and the sold-out API means hosting capacity takes time to spin up. Check in September whether the tools your clients use got noticeably better or cheaper. That is your actual early-warning signal, and it costs you one calendar reminder.
  5. Say it before your client does. "The model behind this is nearly free now — here's why you're paying me anyway" is a strong sentence when you say it first, and a terrible position when they say it first.
REALITY CHECKThis card climbed on velocity, but be careful not to read the noise as the threat — the noise is a symptom, the irreversibility is the threat. The reason this sits at CRITICAL is narrow and specific: open weights are permanent. Every previous price drop in AI was a decision someone could reverse; the July 27 release can't be — a sold-out API today, and even a new peak-hour surcharge, don't change that a free download lands next Monday. There is no tactic that restores scarcity to output; you cannot out-produce free, and you cannot wait it out. The honest response is a repositioning: what you sell has to become the judgment about which output is right, the accountability for the result, and the relationship that survives being wrong once. That is months of work on your offer and your pricing, and it is worth starting in the quarter where you still have leverage. Equally honest in the other direction: this is a floor-under-inputs story, not an "AI replaces you tomorrow" one. Your clients will still not know what to do with the output. That gap is the business — but only if you actually charge for it.

CONTEXT — WHY IT'S THE STRUCTURAL GIANT

This is the assistant clients already have open, running on GPT-5.5 Instant, serving 1.1 billion monthly users — the fastest app in history to that scale. An owner can paste their situation in and get a competent campaign plan, competitor breakdown, coaching reframe, or first-draft strategy in seconds. It doesn't have to beat you — it just has to be good enough that the client questions the gap between your invoice and a subscription. The price lever is the sharpest edge, and today it got sharper on two fronts. First, free frontier-class models: LongCat-2.0 joins DeepSeek V4 and GLM-5.2 as open-weight coders that are near-frontier and cost nothing to license — the "always-cheapest substitute" now includes "free." Second, the pricing critique went mainstream: Karp's "wealth tax" line names the exact dynamic owners feel — that frontier labs charge far above the marginal cost of inference — which accelerates the client conversation about whether the human markup is worth it. Layer GPT-5.6 on top (Sol billed as OpenAI's strongest model yet, GA "coming weeks") and the next raise on "good enough" is still on its way. The share dip below 50% cuts toward owners: clients now have several near-free substitutes, not one — which makes the "why am I paying a human?" question louder, not quieter.

IMPACT — WHAT IT MEANS FOR THE MONEY

Fee compression at the root, hitting all three segments at once. The battleground is the mid-market — startups, agencies, and independent operators willing to route work to whatever is cheapest and good enough — and "cheapest" now includes "free and frontier-class." The 2026 field reports are blunt: one agency owner says a client demanded an 80% fee cut because "they could get better results from ChatGPT," another cut their team 60% to stay profitable, and early-career sales/marketing roles have shed roughly 20% of headcount. Even the top of the market is moving — about a quarter of McKinsey's fees are now outcome-based, and McKinsey has cut 3,000–4,000 roles (its largest reduction since 2008) as AI compresses the analyst-hours behind the work. Brookings' Molly Kinder calls it the "messy middle": most jobs survive, but losses concentrate in exactly the best-paid cognitive office work advisory firms sell. A quiet OpenAI holiday changes none of it; the cheaper-and-freer-tokens trend is doing the damage right now.
